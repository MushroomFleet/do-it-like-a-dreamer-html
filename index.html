01 — The world outside your windshield

One shader,
whole horizon.

Everything moving behind this panel — the dusk gradient, the ridgelines, the road, the twin rivers of tail-lights — is a single WebGL2 fragment shader. No textures. No meshes. No three.js. Roughly two hundred lines of GLSL painting every pixel, every frame, from pure math.

The sky is three color ramps cross-faded in code. The mountains are one fbm() noise function sliced into silhouettes. The road is a one-line perspective divide: z = height / (horizon − y). The tail-lights are Gaussian ridges of noise streamed along that same z.

And it listens. The page hands the shader a journey uniform as you scroll — the sun sets, stars ignite, the whole world rolls from dusk into deep night, and (if you make it to the end) out the other side into dawn. Scroll speed feeds a second uniform: drive faster and the light streaks stretch.

02 — Choreography without a conductor

The scrollbar
is the timeline.

The rev-bar pinned to the top of this page is pure CSS — two declarations: animation-timeline: scroll(root) and a keyframe that scales it. No scroll listener, no JavaScript, no main-thread work at all: the compositor scrubs it directly.

The cards below run on view() timelines — animation progress keyed to each card's own position in your viewport. They aren't triggered, they're scrubbed: drag your scrollbar backwards and time runs in reverse. Entry and exit both, declared in a line each with animation-range.

03 — Paint beyond sRGB

Color that
keeps its nerve.

CHROMA

The word above never stops moving through hue — and never goes muddy — because it's specified in OKLCH, where lightness is perceptual: hue 40 and hue 240 at L 72% genuinely look equally bright, which hex codes have never once promised you.

The cycling itself is a registered custom property — @property --hue { syntax: '<number>' } — which makes the hue itself animatable, so one keyframe drives a three-stop gradient. The ring below interpolates in oklch longer hue, the swatches are stepped around the hue wheel at constant lightness and chroma, and on a wide-gamut display these reds and greens sit outside sRGB entirely — the browser is compositing this page in Display-P3.

04 — Letterforms with a throttle

Type at speed.

The specimen below is not ten fonts. It is one file with two sliders — Archivo's variable axes: weight 100→900 and width 62→125, both continuous. Your scrollbar is holding both sliders: as the word crosses the middle of your viewport it inflates to full width and full weight, then exhales on the way out.

Every headline on this page is the same single font file at a different point on those axes — set with font-variation-settings, animated for free, no extra network weight.

05 — This one goes to eleven

11,000 rpm.

The tachometer below is a 2D canvas redrawn every frame. The needle isn't tweened — it's simulated: a damped spring (stiffness 130, damping 13) chasing the engine. The overshoot and the shiver at redline aren't easing curves; they're physics. The wobble is the point.

The engine note is synthesized live by the Web Audio API — two sawtooth oscillators a firing-order apart, a sub-octave, and band-passed noise for intake hiss, pushed through a tanh waveshaper so it growls instead of buzzes. Pitch tracks rpm at f = rpm / 20. Nothing here is a video and nothing is a sample.
